## Abstract

This document records the architectural decisions that shaped methodology v0.1 of the MakeitSEO Standards Programme. It is not a tutorial or an introduction; for orientation read `methodology-overview/v0.1` first. The ADR exists to answer the question "why is the methodology structured this way?" for future contributors, implementers, and reviewers.

Each decision records: the choice made, the alternatives considered, the rationale, the consequences. Where a decision is provisional or evidence-conditional, the conditions for revisiting are explicit. Deferred decisions are listed separately with the criteria that would resolve them.

The ADR is authoritative for "why" questions. If the spec set contradicts the ADR on a structural matter, the ADR wins and the offending spec should be amended. Where the spec set contradicts the ADR on a tactical matter (specific weights, specific thresholds), the spec wins; the ADR is the architecture-level record.

## Status

This is the v0.1 ADR. It will be amended as v0.2 and v1.0 ship. Amendments preserve historical decision text; new decisions are appended rather than replacing old ones, so the chronology of architectural reasoning remains visible.

## Scope

### In scope

- Architectural decisions about pillar composition, scoring rules, governance, licensing
- Decisions about how ARC measurement-vocabulary specs relate to outcome pillar specs
- Decisions about derived cross-cutting views
- Decisions deferred to v0.2 with their resolution criteria

### Out of scope

- Tactical decisions about specific sub-score weights, specific thresholds, specific data adapter choices (these live in the per-spec methodology files)
- Operational decisions about cron schedules, tier limits, customer-facing UX (these live in the implementation reference)
- Vendor selection decisions about third-party APIs (these are operational rather than architectural)

## Decisions

### Decision 1 — Three outcome pillars

**Decision:** The methodology has three outcome pillars: SEO, AEO, GEO. Each measures a distinct customer outcome.

- **SEO** measures traditional search ranking
- **AEO** measures deterministic answer surface inclusion
- **GEO** measures generative AI citation

**Alternatives considered:**
- Two pillars (SEO + GEO with AEO folded into one or the other)
- Four pillars (SEO + AEO + GEO + ARC as a peer pillar)
- A single composite "AI-era search" score

**Rationale:** The three pillars measure outcomes that are commercially distinct. A site can score 80 on SEO and 30 on GEO without internal contradiction — the picture is real. Folding them together obscures structural issues; treating them separately exposes them. The four-pillar alternative is addressed in Decision 2.

**Consequences:** The methodology surface area is broader than single-score competitors. Customer-facing UI must present three numbers + the overall mean. Implementation must produce three independent scoring services. Decision 7 documents the independence rule.

### Decision 2 — ARC is a measurement vocabulary, not a pillar

**Decision:** Agent Runtime Compatibility (ARC) is treated as a measurement-vocabulary spec used by the three outcome pillars, not as a fourth outcome pillar.

**Alternatives considered:**
- ARC as a fourth pillar composing into the overall score (initial methodology design assumption)
- ARC as an entirely separate "operational health" surface, divorced from the outcome pillars

**Rationale:** Runtime compatibility is a precondition rather than an outcome. It does not produce a distinct customer-facing surface (you don't "get cited on ARC"); it determines whether the outcome surfaces are reachable at all. Folding it into the three outcome pillars where its failure modes land produces a more honest measurement — a CSR-rendered site's runtime invisibility shows up directly in GEO sub-score 8 (runtime compatibility) rather than as a separate score the customer might dismiss.

The reframing was made during the methodology design session in May 2026. The initial four-pillar framing had ARC as a peer pillar; reflection on what ARC actually measures (runtime readability) versus what the outcome pillars measure (customer outcomes) clarified that ARC is the wrong shape for a pillar.

**Consequences:** ARC findings route to pillar runtime sub-scores via Decision 3. The cross-cutting Agent Runtime Readiness view (and later, the Retrievability view in Decision 15) surfaces ARC measurements at a single display location for diagnostic clarity, without composing into the overall score.

### Decision 3 — ARC findings route to pillars via `relevant_outcome_pillar`

**Decision:** Each runtime in the ARC registry declares which outcome pillar(s) its findings contribute to via a `relevant_outcome_pillar: PillarId[]` characteristic. Each pillar's runtime sub-score is computed by aggregating findings from runtimes routed to it.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Hard-coded routing in scoring code (per-runtime if/else statements)
- Per-finding routing tags (each finding declares its target pillar)
- Single shared "runtime compatibility" sub-score across all three pillars

**Rationale:** The `relevant_outcome_pillar` characteristic is data, not code. New runtimes added at v0.2 declare their routing without scoring-code changes. The per-runtime characteristic also captures the genuine multi-pillar nature of some runtimes (Cloudflare Project Think affects both AEO and GEO; Google Search-as-Agent-Manager affects both SEO and AEO).

Per-finding routing tags were considered but rejected: it would require every check to know its pillar, which is the wrong layering. Findings should be runtime-property measurements; pillar routing should be a runtime-level characteristic.

**Consequences:** Multi-pillar runtimes route to multiple pillars with even-distribution at v0.1. Per-pillar routing-weight tuning is deferred to v0.2 (see Deferred 4). The seeded routing values for the six v0.1 canonical runtimes are documented in `agent-runtime-compatibility/v0.1`.

### Decision 4 — Version-per-path convention

**Decision:** Each spec versions independently via path-based versioning. The canonical URL of a spec is `<spec-name>/<version>.md`. v0.2 of a spec lives at `<spec-name>/v0.2.md`, not replacing `<spec-name>/v0.1.md`.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Single repository version (every spec ships at the same version, advance together)
- File-replacement versioning (newer version overwrites older at the same path)

**Rationale:** Each spec evolves on its own timeline. ARC will likely refresh more frequently than methodology-overview because new runtimes ship; the version-per-path convention lets ARC reach v0.3 while methodology-overview is still at v0.1. File-replacement breaks the principle that historical findings should remain interpretable in the context of the version they were produced under.

**Consequences:** Findings carry `methodology_ref = 'spec-name/version'` (per Decision 5) and remain interpretable across version updates. The repository accumulates spec files over time without churn at the path level.

### Decision 5 — Every finding carries a `methodology_ref`

**Decision:** Every finding produced by an implementation of the standard carries a `methodology_ref` field declaring which spec version(s) produced it. The format is `spec-name/version`. Multi-spec findings use `+`-separation: `brand-web-mentions/v0.1+geo-scoring/v0.1`.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Implementation-level versioning (whole platform versioned; per-finding tagging unnecessary)
- No versioning at all

**Rationale:** Per Decision 4, specs version independently. A finding produced in May 2026 under a v0.1 spec remains interpretable in November 2026 even after the spec advances. Without per-finding versioning, the meaning of historical findings drifts as specs evolve.

The dual-reference convention (`spec-a/v0.1+spec-b/v0.1`) captures findings produced by overlapping specs — a brand-mentions finding consumed by GEO scoring carries both refs because both specs governed the measurement.

**Consequences:** Database schemas include `methodology_ref TEXT NOT NULL` columns on findings tables. Customer-facing UI annotates findings with their methodology version where the customer cares (typically only when re-score-on-read is invoked). Implementation reference documents the canonical formatting rules.

### Decision 6 — Overall score is the arithmetic mean of three pillar scores

**Decision:** The overall MakeitSEO score is `(seo_pillar_score + aeo_pillar_score + geo_pillar_score) / 3`. No pillar-level weighting.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Weighted mean with hand-picked weights (e.g. SEO 40%, AEO 30%, GEO 30%)
- Weighted mean with operator-tunable weights per customer
- Cohort-calibrated weights derived from customer outcome data
- No overall score (display three pillar numbers; let customers interpret)

**Rationale:** The arithmetic mean has a built-in defence: we measure three outcomes, all are commercially important, the evidence base does not yet support a confident claim that one outcome intrinsically matters more than the others. Unequal weighting would require defending specific numbers; the 2026 evidence base does not yet supply those numbers.

Operator-tunable weights would create per-customer score incomparability — Customer A's 75 means something different than Customer B's 75. Cohort-calibrated weights are attractive but the cohort doesn't exist yet at launch.

The "no overall score" alternative was considered briefly. It was rejected because customers and journalists want a single number to anchor on. Refusing to provide one is a position that ages poorly when every competitor product offers a single score.

**Consequences:** Sub-score weights within each pillar are evidence-grounded and may evolve at v0.2 against cohort data. Pillar-level weights are not, so they are equal. Deferred 1 documents the conditions under which pillar weighting may be introduced.

### Decision 7 — Pillar independence

**Decision:** The three pillar scores are scored independently. No pillar's score is derived from, constrained by, or post-adjusted using another pillar's score.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Pillar interaction terms (e.g. low SEO score reduces GEO score if traffic is needed for citation)
- Sequential gating (e.g. AEO score capped at SEO score because answer surfaces require ranked content first)

**Rationale:** Pillar independence is the central methodological commitment of the framework. It prevents the framework from hiding structural issues behind aggregate scoring. A site that ranks well but is invisible to AI engines should score high on SEO and low on GEO honestly; smoothing the asymmetry obscures the customer's actual position.

The cascade-weighting question (Deferred 2) is a possible exception to this rule. If cohort data shows that ARC failures genuinely depress citation outcomes beyond what the GEO runtime sub-score already captures, then a between-pillar interaction term may be justified at v0.2. v0.1 maintains strict independence as the safe default.

**Consequences:** Each pillar's scoring service is independent. No cross-pillar code dependencies in the scoring layer. The overall score (Decision 6) is the only cross-pillar interaction at v0.1.

### Decision 8 — FAQ schema is not an AEO signal at v0.1

**Decision:** FAQ schema (`FAQPage` and related schema.org types) is not measured as an AEO sub-score at v0.1. HowTo schema is similarly excluded.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Retain FAQ schema as an AEO sub-score at reduced weight (anticipate partial rich-result return)
- Move FAQ schema to GEO scoring as a structured-content contributor
- Remove FAQ schema from all scoring

**Rationale:** Google deprecated FAQ rich results for all sites on 7 May 2026 (notice published 6 May, deprecation effective from then). The previous AEO methodology weighted FAQ schema based on its rich-result outcome; that outcome no longer exists for the vast majority of sites. Continuing to score FAQ as an AEO signal would mean awarding points for a contribution that produces no measurable outcome.

FAQ-structured content remains useful for AI citation (the GEO pillar's sub-score 6 captures multi-schema + structured content density, which includes FAQ-structured Q&A pages). The choice was to move FAQ-structured content into GEO scoring rather than remove it entirely.

**Consequences:** AEO scoring at v0.1 has five sub-scores, none of them FAQ-related. GEO scoring captures FAQ-structured content at modest weight as part of sub-score 6. If Google reverses the deprecation, the AEO methodology revisits this at v0.2.

### Decision 9 — Brand web mentions are a dual-pillar signal

**Decision:** Brand web mentions are measured once and scored into both SEO (at 3%) and GEO (at 15%). The weights differ to reflect the evidence base, but the measurement is shared.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Single-pillar contribution (GEO only)
- Two separate measurements (one for each pillar, with different methodology)
- Equal weight in both pillars

**Rationale:** The 2026 evidence base supports brand mentions as a strong predictor of AI citation (r=0.664) and a modest signal for traditional ranking (Google March 2026 Core Update recognised it as an authority signal alongside backlinks). The asymmetric weighting honours this. Two measurements with different methodology would create internal inconsistency; a single measurement consumed at different weights preserves coherence.

**Consequences:** The `brand_mention_snapshots` table is populated once per measurement cycle and read by both SEO and GEO scoring services. Each pillar's brand mentions sub-score is computed independently from the shared snapshot. Decision 10 establishes the same pattern for content freshness.

### Decision 10 — Content freshness is a dual-pillar primary signal

**Decision:** Content freshness is measured once and scored into both SEO (at 10%) and GEO (at 10%). The weights are equal because both pillars treat freshness as a meaningful contributor at v0.1.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Single-pillar treatment (GEO only)
- SEO-only treatment (the long-established freshness signal in Google ranking)
- Asymmetric weighting (heavier in GEO per the ConvertMate 3.2× multiplier evidence)

**Rationale:** Freshness affects both outcomes. The ConvertMate finding (3.2× multiplier for content under 30 days for AI citation) is strong evidence for GEO. The long-established Google freshness weighting for time-sensitive queries is strong evidence for SEO. Equal weighting at v0.1 is an honest assessment that both effects are real and approximately comparable.

The shared measurement infrastructure (`page_freshness_snapshots`) means a customer's freshness is computed once and applied to both pillars. The customer cannot "fix freshness for SEO but not GEO" — that asymmetry would be artificial.

**Consequences:** v0.2 may re-weight freshness asymmetrically if cohort data shows a clear difference. The dual-pillar architecture supports this without methodology rework.

### Decision 11 — Per-platform source affinity is outcome-based, not proxy-based

**Decision:** Per-platform source affinity (GEO sub-score 5) is measured by actual citation rate per platform compared to the platform's baseline, not by proxy signals (Wikipedia presence as ChatGPT proxy, Reddit presence as Perplexity proxy, etc.).

**Alternatives considered:**
- Proxy-signal measurement (Wikipedia/Reddit/YouTube/blog presence per platform)
- Mixed measurement (proxy signals as predictors + outcome measurements as ground truth)
- Single platform-agnostic off-domain measurement

**Rationale:** Outcome measurement is more direct than proxy measurement. The customer cares about being cited on ChatGPT, not about whether they have a Wikipedia page that might be cited on ChatGPT. The 615× variation in citation patterns across platforms (Superlines March 2026) is a real signal; measuring the outcome captures it directly.

The proxy approach was the initial methodology design assumption. During Sprint 2.14 implementation, the actual citation-rate measurement was implemented (per `per-platform-source-affinity/v0.1`). The methodology spec was updated to match the implementation, which is the better methodology.

**Consequences:** v0.1 measures actual per-platform citation rate against platform baseline. The proxy signals (Wikipedia entity presence, Reddit mention volume, YouTube channel presence, topical blog mentions) may be re-introduced at v0.2 as auxiliary inputs alongside outcome measurement if cohort data shows they improve prediction. v0.1 keeps the methodology lean.

### Decision 12 — Local SEO is a conditional sub-score weighted at 32%

**Decision:** For customers classified as local-intent, the SEO pillar receives a conditional Local SEO sub-score weighted at 32%, with the non-local sub-scores reduced proportionally. Non-local customers do not receive the Local SEO sub-score; their SEO pillar uses the standard six-sub-score weight distribution.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Local SEO as a fourth outcome pillar (separate from SEO)
- Local SEO as a sub-score added to all customers (zero for non-local)
- Local SEO as a separate scoring module the customer optionally enables

**Rationale:** Local SEO is a specialised application of SEO methodology, not a separate outcome. A local-intent customer's "search ranking" outcome is local pack inclusion; the SEO pillar should capture this rather than treating it as a separate concern. The 32% weighting reflects the 2026 industry consensus (ClickRank Local SEO Ranking Factors 2026: GBP signals 32%, on-page 19%, reviews 16%, links 15%, behavioural 8%, citations 7% — note the 32% number reflects the local-pack-specific signal weight, mapped onto a single SEO sub-score for v0.1).

A separate fourth pillar was rejected because it would double-count signals (the local SEO content sub-score and the general SEO content quality sub-score would overlap). Conditional sub-score with proportional weight redistribution is cleaner.

**Consequences:** SEO scoring has two weight distributions depending on local-intent classification (per `seo-scoring/v0.1`). The local-intent flag is captured during onboarding and operator-overridable. Multi-classification customers (operating across local and national channels) are handled by operator primary-classification at v0.1; a formal blended scoring framework is deferred to v0.2.

### Decision 13 — Editor-led governance at v0.1; Stage 2 at ~6-8 weeks post-launch

**Decision:** At v0.1, the methodology is governed by Daniel Titton as editor (Cloud WiFi Limited). Substantive changes are made by the editor with input from external contributors via GitHub Issues. Stage 2 governance (technical advisory board, multi-stakeholder review for substantive changes) is targeted for ~6-8 weeks after methodology v0.1 launches.

**Alternatives considered:**
- Multi-stakeholder governance from day one (W3C-style consortium)
- Permanent editor-led governance (no Stage 2 transition)
- Vendor-controlled governance (Cloud WiFi proprietary methodology under open license)

**Rationale:** Multi-stakeholder governance is too heavy for v0.1 when the spec set is still settling. Editor-led governance is faster, allows the architect to make consistent decisions across specs, and reflects the reality that the programme is currently a single-author work product. Stage 2 transition is committed in advance to signal that the methodology will not remain a single-author work product indefinitely — it will be governed by a broader community as adoption justifies.

**Consequences:** v0.1 changes are made by the editor. Stage 2 transition involves recruiting a technical advisory board (typically 5-7 members across SEO/AEO/GEO/ARC domains), establishing a substantive-change review process, and migrating governance documentation. Stage 2 is documented in `GOVERNANCE.md` in the standards repo.

### Decision 14 — CC-BY 4.0 for specs; MIT for validator

**Decision:** All v0.1 specification documents are published under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International (CC-BY 4.0). The reference implementation validator (`@makeitseo/standard` npm package) is published under the MIT license.

**Alternatives considered:**
- CC0 (public domain) for specs
- CC-BY-SA (share-alike) for specs
- Proprietary specs under MakeitSEO control
- GPL or Apache for validator

**Rationale:** CC-BY 4.0 maximises adoption while requiring attribution. Adoption is the strategic priority for a standards programme — the more implementations, the more durable the methodology becomes. CC-BY's attribution requirement preserves the programme's identity without restricting commercial use. CC0 was considered but the attribution requirement provides modest defensive value (a competitor citing the methodology must say "MakeitSEO Standard," which is good for the brand).

MIT for the validator matches the prevailing convention in the JavaScript ecosystem and aligns with the dev tools the validator integrates with.

**Consequences:** Implementations including commercial products and competitor tools may adopt the methodology under attribution. Competitor-product listing in `implementations/README.md` follows the same attribution rule.

### Decision 15 — Retrievability is a derived cross-pillar view

**Decision:** Methodology v0.1 exposes "Retrievability" as a derived diagnostic view computed from existing sub-scores. It is reported alongside the three pillar scores and the Agent Runtime Readiness view. It does not compose into any pillar score; it does not compose into the overall MakeitSEO score; it is not a new sub-score within any pillar.

**Composition:** Retrievability is the arithmetic mean of:

- The Agent Runtime Readiness view (itself a derived cross-pillar view of ARC runtime-readability sub-scores); and
- The AI crawler accessibility sub-score from the GEO pillar (sub-score 7).

```
retrievability_view = (
  agent_runtime_readiness +
  geo.ai_crawler_accessibility_subscore
) / 2
```

The view is presented on the customer dashboard alongside the three pillar scores. The view is also displayed within the SEO pillar's customer-facing UI as relevant context, because retrievability is increasingly material to SEO outcomes as the search-engine candidate-set width responds to hardware-economics shifts in retrieval cost (see references in `methodology-overview/v0.1` and the Jeffrey 2026 Search Engine Land coverage).

**Alternatives considered:**
- Promote Retrievability to a fourth outcome pillar
- Add Retrievability as a new SEO sub-score (e.g. 5%, with other SEO sub-scores reduced proportionally)
- Leave Retrievability unnamed at v0.1; defer the cross-cutting view to v0.2
- Relabel ARC's existing SEO contribution as "Retrievability" without architectural change

**Rationale:** Industry usage of "Retrievability" coalesced after the four primary specs of methodology v0.1 were authored. The concept overlaps roughly two-thirds with ARC (runtime readability — can the runtime fetch and parse the page?) and one-third with GEO sub-score 7 (crawler permission — is the crawler allowed?). Promoting Retrievability to a new pillar would duplicate ARC's measurement framework. Promoting it to a new SEO sub-score would either require weight redistribution across the existing SEO sub-scores (a methodology change unjustified by current evidence) or be additive to 100% (mathematically invalid). The derived view honours the industry framing without restructuring the architecture.

The fourth-alternative (relabel ARC's existing contribution) was rejected because Retrievability extends ARC: it adds the AI crawler accessibility component, which ARC does not measure. The derived view is the correct shape — it composes existing measurements without overstating their scope.

**Consequences:** The Retrievability view requires no schema change, no migration, no new data adapter. It is computed at audit-view time from existing data (Agent Runtime Readiness + GEO sub-score 7). Sprint 2.15 Phase 4 implements the view. The implementation cost is small (~2 hours of UI work).

**Forward path:** v0.2 may extend Retrievability with a third component — actual fetch evidence from server-log analysis showing AI crawlers visiting the customer's pages (per the recommendation in Jeffrey, 2026, "Google may be about to widen the SEO playing field"). The data adapter for this is not in v0.1; it is identified as Deferred 5.

## Deferred decisions

These are decisions that were considered during methodology v0.1 design but were deferred for resolution at v0.2 or later. Each carries an explicit resolution criterion so the deferral does not become permanent ambiguity.

### Deferred 1 — Pillar-level weighting

**Question:** Should the overall MakeitSEO score weight the three pillars (e.g. 40/30/30) rather than averaging them?

**v0.1 position:** Equal weighting (arithmetic mean) per Decision 6.

**Resolution criteria for v0.2:** Cohort data must demonstrate a clear and consistent finding that one pillar outcome materially differs from the others in commercial impact across customer cohorts. The threshold for action: a Pearson correlation between pillar score and customer-reported commercial outcome (revenue, leads, organic traffic value) exceeds 0.6 for one pillar and is below 0.4 for at least one other pillar. Below this threshold, the equal-weighting default is retained at v0.2.

**Data feed:** Sprint 2.14 onward captures audit-score-to-outcome data on a per-customer basis where customers opt in to share commercial-outcome data. The 60- and 90-day post-launch review window evaluates against this data.

**Review timeline:** Initial review at 60 days post-launch; final v0.2 weighting decision at 90 days.

### Deferred 2 — Cascade-weighting between ARC and citation outcomes

**Question:** Should ARC runtime failures additionally depress citation outcomes in the GEO pillar beyond the runtime sub-score effect?

**v0.1 position:** Strict pillar independence per Decision 7. ARC findings contribute only to their pillar runtime sub-scores; no cross-sub-score cascade within the GEO pillar.

**Resolution criteria for v0.2:**

- **r > 0.5** between ARC failures and GEO citation drops across customer cohorts → introduce cascade weighting in v0.2 (cascade-weighting formula to be designed at the v0.2 review)
- **r < 0.3** → retain status quo; document negative finding in v0.2 ADR
- **0.3 ≤ r ≤ 0.5** → defer further to v0.3 with continued data collection

**Data feed:** Sprint 2.14 onward captures time-paired ARC × citation data per `cascade-monitoring/v0.1`. The implementation provides an admin route for the correlation analysis at the review milestones.

**Review timeline:** Initial review at 60 days post-launch with whatever data exists; final decision at 90 days post-launch.

This deferred decision is the principal v0.2 review item. The data collection infrastructure was a substantial part of Sprint 2.14 because the v0.2 decision depends on continuous time-paired data from launch onwards.

### Deferred 3 — Fourth ARC runtime category

**Question:** Should ARC introduce a fourth runtime category (beyond `browser-execution`, `headless-fetch`, `model-native-harness`) at v0.2?

**v0.1 position:** Three runtime categories. The candidates being monitored are `interactive-browser` (multi-step navigation agents) and `crawler-cached` (cached-index retrieval rather than live fetch).

**Resolution criteria for v0.2:**

A new category is added if a genuinely distinct architectural pattern surfaces with:

- (a) Two or more independently-operated runtimes implementing the pattern as their dominant architecture
- (b) Measurably different readability requirements compared to existing categories
- (c) At least 60 days of observed differentiation from the existing categories

If no candidate meets all three criteria by the v0.2 review, the three-category taxonomy is retained.

**Data feed:** Operator monitoring of new agent runtime launches; ARC registry maintenance pass at the 60-day mark.

**Review timeline:** Reviewed at the v0.2 milestone (~60-90 days post-launch).

### Deferred 4 — Per-runtime routing weights for multi-pillar runtimes

**Question:** Should runtimes routed to multiple pillars (via `relevant_outcome_pillar` arrays of length > 1) have operator-tunable routing weights rather than v0.1's default of even distribution?

**v0.1 position:** Even distribution. A runtime routed to `['aeo', 'geo']` contributes 50% to each pillar's runtime sub-score.

**Resolution criteria for v0.2:** Operator experience with v0.1 shows that even-distribution misrepresents the actual outcome contribution for specific multi-pillar runtimes. If operators report a need for asymmetric weighting (e.g. "Google AIO citation-side runtime affects GEO more than AEO in our customer cohort"), v0.2 introduces an operator-configurable per-runtime per-pillar weight.

**Data feed:** Operator feedback during the first 60 days; cohort-level analysis of how multi-pillar runtime findings correlate with each pillar's outcomes.

**Review timeline:** Reviewed at the v0.2 milestone.

### Deferred 5 — Server-log fetch-evidence extension to Retrievability

**Question:** Should the Retrievability derived view (Decision 15) extend to include actual fetch evidence — whether AI crawlers are observed visiting the customer's pages in server logs over a recent window?

**v0.1 position:** Retrievability measures runtime readability and crawler permission. Actual fetch evidence is not measured.

**Resolution criteria for v0.2:**

The extension ships at v0.2 if:

- (a) A server-log analysis data adapter is feasible to implement (most likely via Cloudflare Analytics for the customer cohort on Cloudflare, with fallback to operator-uploaded log analysis for non-Cloudflare customers)
- (b) Customer demand for the extension is evidenced (customer feedback during the 60-day post-launch window asks for "are AI crawlers actually visiting my site?")
- (c) The extension does not require methodology change beyond adding the third Retrievability component

If feasibility, demand, and methodology stability all hold, the extension ships at v0.2.

**Data feed:** Operator monitoring of customer feedback during the post-launch window.

**Review timeline:** Reviewed at the v0.2 milestone alongside Deferred 2 and Deferred 3.
