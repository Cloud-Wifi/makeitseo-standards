## Abstract

This specification defines the measurement methodology for the SEO (Search Engine Optimisation) pillar in the MakeitSEO Standards Programme. SEO measures **traditional search ranking outcomes** on Google, Bing, and regional search engines — the blue-link visibility a customer earns for tracked queries. Ranking position is the customer outcome SEO tracks.

SEO is one of three outcome pillars in the MakeitSEO Standards Programme, alongside AEO (deterministic answer surfaces) and GEO (generative AI citation). The three pillars compose to the overall MakeitSEO score as the arithmetic mean of their independent pillar scores. The architectural reasoning is captured in `methodology-adr/v0.1`.

SEO v0.1 is the smallest of the three pillar specs by methodological footprint. Traditional SEO ranking factors have been studied for two decades; the v0.1 spec codifies the consensus 2026 understanding rather than introducing novel methodology. Three adjustments distinguish v0.1 from a pre-2026 SEO scoring framework:

1. **Brand web mentions are added as a modest SEO sub-score.** Google's March 2026 Core Update confirmed what practitioners had been observing: unstructured brand mentions now contribute to authority signals alongside traditional backlinks. The sub-score is light (3% weight) because SEO outcomes still depend principally on intent-match, content quality, technical health, and traditional link authority — but the signal is real and gets recognised.
2. **Content freshness is promoted to a first-class sub-score.** Earlier framings buried freshness inside content quality. v0.1 surfaces it explicitly because the dual-pillar treatment (also in GEO) requires shared measurement infrastructure and clear weight attribution.
3. **Runtime compatibility for search crawlers is added.** Routes ARC findings for runtimes tagged with `relevant_outcome_pillar = 'seo'` or `'multiple'` (with SEO in the routing list). Principal SEO-relevant runtimes are Googlebot and Bingbot.

These adjustments are deliberately small. The honest position is that traditional SEO works the way it has worked, with modest 2026 evolution. v0.1 reflects that rather than overclaiming a revolution in SEO scoring.

## Definitions

### Search engine

A web service that responds to user queries with a ranked list of links to web pages, with optional augmentation by features (snippets, knowledge panels, AI overviews, etc.). As of May 2026 the principal search engines for global English-language audiences are Google and Bing, with regional engines (Yandex, Baidu, Naver, etc.) significant for their respective markets.

For SEO pillar scoring, "ranking" refers specifically to the organic blue-link position. Features that augment the SERP (featured snippets, AI Overviews, knowledge panels, PAA) are AEO surfaces and are measured in the AEO pillar. AI citation surfaces are GEO measurements.

### Ranking position

The numerical position at which a customer's URL appears in the organic results for a tracked query. Position 1 is the top organic result; positions 2–10 are page-one results; positions 11+ are page-two-or-later results. Ranking position is the most direct outcome measurement in SEO.

### Intent satisfaction

The degree to which a page satisfies the user's underlying intent for a query, as evaluated by the search engine through its ranking algorithms and quality-evaluation systems. In 2026 Google's framing, intent satisfaction depth is the primary ranking lever — "did the page solve the user's problem in one visit?" Pages that align with how users think, decide, and act at the exact moment of the query outperform pages built only around keyword type.

### E-E-A-T

Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness — Google's framework for evaluating content quality and source credibility. E-E-A-T is not a single score; it's a bundle of signals that quality-evaluation systems use to rank pages. Author attribution, named credentials, citations to authoritative sources, and consistent factual accuracy all contribute.

### Core Web Vitals

Google's set of page-experience metrics: Largest Contentful Paint (LCP), Interaction to Next Paint (INP, replacing FID in 2024), Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS). Core Web Vitals function as a technical floor — sites failing the metrics are penalised but sites passing don't gain ranking advantage proportional to their pass-margin.

### SEO outcome

A measured event where the customer's URL ranks for a tracked query at a tracked position. Outcomes are recorded per query, per search engine, per location (where local intent applies), per timestamp.

### SEO sub-score

One of the five components of the SEO pillar score at v0.1. Each sub-score measures a specific dimension of SEO performance with an explicit weight that sums to 100% across sub-scores.

### Pillar runtime sub-score

The runtime-compatibility component of the SEO sub-score set, populated by routing ARC findings from runtimes tagged with `relevant_outcome_pillar = 'seo'` or `'multiple'` (with SEO in the routing list). Specified in `agent-runtime-compatibility/v0.1`.

## Sub-scores and weights

SEO v0.1 defines five sub-scores. Weights reflect consensus 2026 understanding of what drives traditional ranking.

| Sub-score | Weight | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| Content quality and intent match | 30% | Depth of intent satisfaction, content comprehensiveness, freshness of factual content |
| Technical health | 25% | Core Web Vitals, indexability, crawlability, site architecture, mobile performance |
| Authority signals (links + mentions) | 22% | Backlink profile quality and quantity, plus modest brand-mention contribution |
| Content freshness | 10% | Recency and update cadence of tracked pages (dual-pillar with GEO) |
| Runtime compatibility (search crawlers) | 8% | Whether search crawlers can fully render and index the site |
| Brand web mentions | 3% | Unstructured brand mentions across the open web (dual-pillar with GEO) |
| Local SEO signals (conditional) | up to 25% (replacing content/technical proportionally) | GBP signals, NAP consistency, local citations, reviews — only applied for local-intent businesses |

**Total: 100%** for non-local businesses. Local businesses receive a different sub-score composition (see Local SEO Adjustments section below).

The weight distribution reflects three judgements:

1. **Content quality and intent match dominate (30%).** The 2026 Google ranking system rewards pages that satisfy intent depth. No other single signal carries comparable weight.
2. **Technical health is a floor, not a differentiator (25%).** Sites failing Core Web Vitals or with indexability problems are heavily penalised; sites passing don't gain proportional advantage. The 25% weight ensures the penalty is meaningful without overweighting a non-discriminating signal.
3. **Authority signals carry 25% combined (22% + 3%).** Traditional backlinks remain meaningful but their relative weight has compressed as quality-evaluation systems have matured. Brand mentions are explicitly weighted at 3% because the 2026 Core Update recognised them as authority signals alongside backlinks; the weight is modest because their direct SEO impact is smaller than their GEO impact.

### Note on dual-pillar signals

Two sub-scores appear in both SEO and GEO with different weights:

- **Content freshness:** 10% in SEO, 10% in GEO. Same measurement, dual pillar consequence.
- **Brand web mentions:** 3% in SEO, 15% in GEO. Same measurement, weighted asymmetrically because the 2026 evidence base supports stronger correlation with AI citation (r=0.664) than with search ranking.

Dual-pillar weighting is not double-counting; it reflects that the same measurement legitimately affects two outcomes that are scored independently. The implementation shares the underlying measurement adapters; only the weighting differs.

## Sub-score methodology

### 1. Content quality and intent match (30% of SEO pillar)

**What it measures:** how well the customer's content satisfies the user's intent for tracked queries, evaluated through:

- Content depth: word count appropriate to query intent, comprehensiveness of coverage
- Intent format match: informational queries served by informational content, transactional by transactional, navigational by navigational
- Topical authority: depth of coverage across related topics in the customer's category
- E-E-A-T signals: author attribution, credentials, named experts, citations to authoritative sources
- Readability and content structure: heading hierarchy, paragraph structure, internal navigation

**Method:**

1. The audit pipeline parses tracked pages for content depth signals (word count, heading structure, internal links, citations).
2. For each tracked query, the pipeline assesses intent match by comparing the customer's serving page against the top three organic results for the query (DataForSEO SERP API).
3. E-E-A-T signals are detected through schema (Person, Article with author), named entity recognition for credentials, and citation/reference parsing.
4. The sub-score combines content depth, intent match, topical authority, and E-E-A-T into a single 0–100 number per the methodology in the implementation reference.

**Why 30%:** intent satisfaction depth is consistently described in 2026 Google guidance as the primary ranking driver. No other single signal carries comparable weight in the published research.

### 2. Technical health (25% of SEO pillar)

**What it measures:** the technical health of the customer's site as evaluated by:

- Core Web Vitals (LCP, INP, CLS) on mobile and desktop
- Indexability and crawlability (robots.txt, meta robots, internal linking)
- Site architecture (URL structure, breadcrumbs, sitemap)
- HTTPS and security
- Mobile responsiveness and mobile-first considerations
- Render-blocking resources and page weight

**Method:**

1. The audit pipeline runs Lighthouse or equivalent on tracked pages, capturing Core Web Vitals plus secondary technical signals.
2. Technical issues are categorised by severity: critical (blocks indexing), high (affects ranking), moderate (best practice), low (edge cases).
3. The sub-score is computed as 100 minus the weighted sum of detected issues, with critical issues weighted heaviest.

**Why 25%:** technical health is a threshold rather than a continuous discriminator. Sites in the failure range (slow LCP, high CLS, indexability problems) suffer meaningful ranking penalty. Sites in the passing range don't gain proportional advantage. The 25% weight ensures the penalty is real without rewarding marginal optimisation past the threshold.

### 3. Authority signals (links + mentions) (22% of SEO pillar)

**What it measures:** the customer's traditional backlink profile, weighted by source quality. The brand-mention contribution at 3% is split out as a separate sub-score (sub-score 6) for measurement clarity, but the conceptual category "authority signals" encompasses both.

**Method:**

1. The audit pipeline queries DataForSEO Backlinks API for the customer's backlink profile.
2. Each backlink is weighted by source domain authority, anchor text relevance, link context, and link freshness.
3. The aggregate is normalised to a 0–100 score with log-scale dampening (so a profile with 10,000 backlinks doesn't score dramatically higher than one with 1,000 high-quality backlinks).
4. Negative signals (toxic backlinks, paid link patterns, link schemes) reduce the score; the sub-score includes a disavow-recommendation finding when toxic-link clusters are detected.

**Why 22%:** traditional backlinks remain a meaningful ranking factor in 2026 but their relative weight has compressed. 22% reflects this — significant but no longer dominant.

### 4. Content freshness (10% of SEO pillar)

**What it measures:** the recency and update cadence of the customer's tracked pages.

**Method:**

Shared with GEO. The audit pipeline records `datePublished` and `dateModified` per page; freshness is computed with the multiplier curve specified in `content-freshness/v0.1`. The aggregate is normalised to a 0–100 score, weighted by page importance.

**Why 10%:** Google explicitly weights freshness for time-sensitive queries (news, current events, evolving topics). For evergreen content, freshness matters less. The 10% weight reflects the average; pages serving genuinely time-sensitive queries see freshness contribute more, evergreen pages see it contribute less, but the pillar weight is uniform.

### 5. Runtime compatibility (search crawlers) (8% of SEO pillar)

**What it measures:** whether search engine crawlers can fully render and index the customer's site. Computed by routing ARC compatibility findings from runtimes tagged with `relevant_outcome_pillar = 'seo'` or `'multiple'` (with SEO in the routing list).

**Method:**

1. The runtime registry identifies SEO-relevant runtimes: Googlebot, Bingbot, regional crawler equivalents.
2. The audit pipeline executes ARC compatibility checks against each SEO-relevant runtime.
3. Findings are aggregated per the standard ARC pattern.
4. Findings are routed to SEO via the runtime's `relevant_outcome_pillar` characteristic.

**Why 8%:** search crawlers in 2026 are mature. Googlebot renders JavaScript reliably; Bingbot has improved JS handling significantly. CSR-only architectures are still suboptimal for SEO but less penalising than they were five years ago. The 8% weight reflects that most modern sites pass search-crawler compatibility cleanly, so the sub-score rarely moves the needle for the customer-facing pillar score.

### 6. Brand web mentions (3% of SEO pillar)

**What it measures:** unstructured brand mentions across the open web. Shared measurement with GEO sub-score 2; weighted lower in SEO because the direct ranking impact is smaller than the AI citation impact.

**Method:**

Shared with GEO. The DataForSEO Backlinks API unlinked-mention endpoint provides the data; quality weighting follows the same pattern. The aggregate is normalised to a 0–100 score.

**Why 3%:** the March 2026 Google Core Update recognised brand mentions as authority signals alongside backlinks but did not elevate them to a primary signal. 3% reflects this — meaningful enough to surface in the pillar score, not significant enough to dominate.

### Note on the Retrievability derived view (cross-pillar)

The SEO pillar's runtime-compatibility sub-score (sub-score 5 at 8%) feeds into a cross-cutting derived view called Retrievability. Retrievability combines ARC runtime-readability measurements across all three pillars with the GEO pillar's AI crawler accessibility sub-score, producing a single diagnostic figure that summarises whether the customer's pages are eligible to enter search and AI candidate sets at all.

Retrievability does not contribute to the SEO pillar score; it is computed independently of pillar scoring and is reported separately. However, the SEO pillar's customer-facing UI surfaces the retrievability view as relevant context. The reasoning: as the search-engine candidate set responds to hardware-economics shifts in retrieval cost (Google's TurboQuant publication in March 2026 signalled that the long-standing 20-30 page candidate-set width may widen substantially), retrievability becomes increasingly material to traditional SEO outcomes. A page that ranks well today within a narrow candidate set may face broader competition once the set widens; content that was retrieval-ready from the start will compete on more even terms.

The retrievability view is fully specified in `methodology-overview/v0.1` and in `methodology-adr/v0.1` Decision 15. It is a v0.1 view; v0.2 is expected to extend it with server-log fetch evidence to measure not just whether crawlers are permitted, but whether they are actually visiting.

The SEO pillar's scoring at v0.1 does not include retrievability as a sub-score because the available evidence base does not yet support a confident weight assignment to forward-state retrieval economics within traditional ranking. The v0.2 review window (60-90 days post-launch) revisits this question on cohort data, per the cascade-monitoring protocol in `methodology-adr/v0.1` Deferred 2.

### Local SEO sub-score (conditional, replacing content/technical weight proportionally)

**Applies to:** customers whose business is identified as local-intent (Wizard Step 3 onboarding captures this via the `service_reach` field).

**What it measures:** local-specific ranking signals:

- Google Business Profile signals (completeness, categories, posts, photos, reviews)
- NAP consistency (Name, Address, Phone) across the web
- Local citation sources (directory listings, regional publications, local chambers of commerce)
- Review signals (review volume, review recency, review sentiment)
- Behavioural signals (clicks, calls, direction requests from GBP)
- Local content signals (location-specific pages, service-area pages)

**Weight distribution for local-intent customers:**

The local-SEO sub-score replaces some of the content quality and technical health weight, reflecting the local-pack ranking signal mix documented for 2026:

| Sub-score | Non-local weight | Local weight |
|---|---|---|
| Content quality and intent match | 30% | 19% |
| Technical health | 25% | 15% |
| Authority signals (links) | 22% | 15% |
| Content freshness | 10% | 8% |
| Runtime compatibility | 8% | 8% |
| Brand web mentions | 3% | 3% |
| **Local SEO signals (new)** | 0% | **32%** |

**Total local: 100%**

The local weight distribution mirrors the 2026 local-ranking-factor consensus (GBP signals 32%, on-page 19%, reviews 16%, links 15%, behavioral 8%, citation signals 7% per ClickRank/SOCi 2026 data).

**Local SEO measurement is specified in:** `local-seo-signals/v0.1` (separate spec). The principal GBP-signal sub-component requires API integration with Google Business Profile.

## Aggregation rule

The SEO pillar score is computed as the weighted sum of the sub-scores, with weights depending on whether the customer is local-intent or non-local.

For non-local businesses:

```
seo_pillar_score = (
  0.30 * content_quality_score +
  0.25 * technical_health_score +
  0.22 * authority_signals_score +
  0.10 * content_freshness_score +
  0.08 * runtime_compat_subscore +
  0.03 * brand_mentions_score
)
```

For local-intent businesses:

```
seo_pillar_score = (
  0.19 * content_quality_score +
  0.15 * technical_health_score +
  0.15 * authority_signals_score +
  0.08 * content_freshness_score +
  0.08 * runtime_compat_subscore +
  0.03 * brand_mentions_score +
  0.32 * local_seo_signals_score
)
```

Each sub-score is on a 0–100 scale. The pillar score is on a 0–100 scale.

### Local vs non-local determination

The `service_reach` field captured in the onboarding wizard (Step 3 — Reach + Locations + Audience) determines local-intent classification. Values:

- `national` or `international` → non-local
- `regional`, `metro`, `local`, `service-area` → local-intent
- Customer-tunable: an operator can manually flag a customer as local-intent even with `national` reach (e.g. a national chain with strong local-pack dependency)

### Independence

The SEO pillar score is independent of AEO and GEO pillar scores. Per `methodology-adr/v0.1` Decision 1, the three pillars measure distinct outcomes and are scored independently.

## Routing of ARC findings into SEO

Per `agent-runtime-compatibility/v0.1`, runtimes in the registry declare a `relevant_outcome_pillar` characteristic. The principal SEO-relevant runtimes at v0.1 are:

| Runtime | Pillar routing | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Googlebot | `multiple: [seo, aeo]` | Googlebot ranks (SEO) and feeds AIO/snippet pipelines (AEO) |
| Bingbot | `seo` | Bing ranking |
| Regional crawlers (Yandex, Baidu, etc.) | `seo` | Regional search ranking |

The `multiple` routing for Googlebot defaults to even distribution (50% SEO, 50% AEO) at v0.1. Operator-tunable routing is deferred to v0.2.

## Implementation guidance

### Data adapters

SEO v0.1 reuses existing data adapters from earlier development. New adapters required for v0.1:

- **DataForSEO Backlinks API — unlinked mention endpoint** (shared with GEO; new at Sprint 2.14)
- **Content freshness measurement** (shared with GEO; new at Sprint 2.14)
- **Local SEO signals** (conditional, for local-intent customers): GBP API integration, NAP consistency parser, local citation scraper. These are scoped for Sprint 2.14 conditionally on local-customer cohort needs.

Existing adapters continuing in use:

- DataForSEO SERP API for ranking position
- DataForSEO Backlinks API for backlink profile
- Lighthouse for Core Web Vitals
- Audit pipeline page parser for content quality signals

### Tracked-query set integration

SEO uses the same tracked-query set as AEO and GEO (Wizard Step 5). The query cap per tier (Starter 50, Growth 200, Scale 500) applies uniformly.

### Methodology version tagging

Every SEO finding records `methodology_ref = 'seo-scoring/v0.1'`. Sub-score findings carrying dual references to sub-specs (e.g. `seo-scoring/v0.1+content-freshness/v0.1`, `seo-scoring/v0.1+brand-web-mentions/v0.1`) are emitted with `+`-separated methodology refs.

### Confidence handling

As with AEO and GEO, sub-scores with thin data are flagged low-confidence. SEO's low-confidence cases are typically transient (new domain, recent migration) rather than structural.

## Edge cases

### Customer is multi-location with mixed local and national presence

A customer with both national reach (e.g. an e-commerce site shipping nationally) and physical locations (e.g. flagship stores) has both local and non-local SEO needs. The implementation supports:

- Primary pillar score computed using the customer's principal classification (operator-configurable)
- Per-location secondary SEO scores computed using local-intent weights, aggregated as a customer-weighted mean
- Customer-facing finding distinguishes "primary national SEO" from "per-location SEO"

The v0.1 spec accommodates this through the local-intent flag override in the wizard. A formal multi-classification framework is deferred to v0.2.

### Customer's site is on a new domain

New domains have structurally low backlink profiles and weak entity recognition. The pillar score reflects this honestly; customer-facing finding contextualises the score against domain-age expectations. The pillar score is not adjusted for domain age; the customer's growth path is normal SEO over time.

### Customer publishes time-sensitive content (news, finance, fast-moving topics)

Time-sensitive content sees freshness contribute more strongly to ranking than the 10% weight suggests at the pillar level (freshness affects content-quality scoring for time-sensitive queries via the underlying Google ranking system). The sub-score weight is uniform; the within-sub-score detection of time-sensitive intent is documented in the implementation reference.

### Customer has heavy international SEO requirements

Customers operating across multiple language and country markets require per-market measurement. The v0.1 pillar score is computed at the customer's primary market level; per-market detail is available via per-market tracked-query sets. Cross-market aggregation rules are deferred to v0.2.

### Customer's site has hreflang and canonical complications

Complex international or large-scale sites with hreflang and canonical patterns are common sources of indexability issues. The technical health sub-score detects and surfaces these issues; the sub-score is honest about the problem without adjusting weight.

## Examples

### Example 1: B2B SaaS with mature SEO position

A mid-market B2B SaaS, national reach, tracked-query set of 80 queries, established backlink profile, mature site.

- Content quality and intent match: comprehensive content, named authors, strong topical authority → `content_quality_score = 78`
- Technical health: Core Web Vitals passing, clean indexability → `technical_health_score = 92`
- Authority signals: solid backlink profile, moderate brand mentions → `authority_signals_score = 70`
- Content freshness: regular blog updates, quarterly key-page refreshes → `content_freshness_score = 75`
- Runtime compatibility: SSR with full crawler access → `runtime_compat_subscore = 100`
- Brand web mentions: 320 quality-weighted mentions (90 days) → `brand_mentions_score = 75`

SEO pillar score (non-local weights):
```
0.30 * 78 + 0.25 * 92 + 0.22 * 70 + 0.10 * 75 + 0.08 * 100 + 0.03 * 75
= 23.4 + 23 + 15.4 + 7.5 + 8 + 2.25
= 79.55
```

The SEO pillar score is 80. Strong across the board with the largest contribution from content quality (23.4) and technical health (23). The clearest improvement lever is authority signals at 70; growing the backlink profile and brand mention volume would lift the pillar.

### Example 2: Local services business with strong GBP

A local services business (regional reach), tracked-query set of 35 queries, strong Google Business Profile.

- Content quality and intent match: location pages well-structured, service descriptions clear → `content_quality_score = 70`
- Technical health: passing CWV, mobile-first clean → `technical_health_score = 88`
- Authority signals: modest backlink profile, local citations strong → `authority_signals_score = 55`
- Content freshness: monthly blog updates → `content_freshness_score = 72`
- Runtime compatibility: SSR clean → `runtime_compat_subscore = 100`
- Brand web mentions: 60 quality-weighted local mentions → `brand_mentions_score = 45`
- **Local SEO signals**: complete GBP, 4.7-star average across 145 reviews, NAP consistent across 18 directories, regular GBP posts → `local_seo_signals_score = 88`

SEO pillar score (local weights):
```
0.19 * 70 + 0.15 * 88 + 0.15 * 55 + 0.08 * 72 + 0.08 * 100 + 0.03 * 45 + 0.32 * 88
= 13.3 + 13.2 + 8.25 + 5.76 + 8 + 1.35 + 28.16
= 78.02
```

The SEO pillar score is 78. The strongest contributor is local SEO signals (28.16); the GBP-driven local-pack performance is doing most of the work. Content quality (13.3) and technical health (13.2) are next; authority signals (8.25) are the clearest improvement lever.

### Example 3: New-domain startup with strong content but weak authority

A startup launched 6 months ago, national reach, tracked-query set of 50 queries, fresh content, minimal backlinks.

- Content quality and intent match: deep founder-authored content, strong topical focus → `content_quality_score = 75`
- Technical health: clean modern stack → `technical_health_score = 90`
- Authority signals: weak backlink profile (12 quality backlinks) → `authority_signals_score = 25`
- Content freshness: weekly updates → `content_freshness_score = 85`
- Runtime compatibility: SSR clean → `runtime_compat_subscore = 100`
- Brand web mentions: 8 quality-weighted mentions → `brand_mentions_score = 15`

SEO pillar score:
```
0.30 * 75 + 0.25 * 90 + 0.22 * 25 + 0.10 * 85 + 0.08 * 100 + 0.03 * 15
= 22.5 + 22.5 + 5.5 + 8.5 + 8 + 0.45
= 67.45
```

The SEO pillar score is 67. The pillar is held down by weak authority signals (5.5) and minimal brand mentions (0.45). The content quality, technical health, and freshness sub-scores are strong but cannot compensate for the structural authority gap. The customer's path forward is link-building and brand-mention growth — a 1–2 year trajectory typical for new-domain SEO.
