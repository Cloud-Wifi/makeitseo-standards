## Abstract

The MakeitSEO Standard is an open methodology for measuring website performance against the realities of search in 2026. Search has split into multiple observable surfaces — traditional ranking, deterministic answer surfaces, generative AI citation — and a new runtime layer that sits between AI models and websites. A unified measurement framework must address all of these without conflating them.

This document is the programme overview. It introduces the methodology architecture, names the constituent specifications, explains how they compose, and points to where the detailed methodology lives. It is the first document an implementer, journalist, or evaluator should read.

The methodology is structured around **three outcome pillars** — SEO, AEO, GEO — composed by arithmetic mean into a single overall score. Agent Runtime Compatibility (ARC) provides the measurement vocabulary that each pillar references for its runtime sub-scores. ARC is not itself a pillar; it is the shared vocabulary that makes runtime measurement consistent across pillars.

The framework is grounded in the 2026 industry evidence base. The single strongest predictor of AI citation is brand web mentions (Ahrefs, r=0.664); the strongest deterministic answer surface is now Google AI Overviews; agent runtimes shipped as a commercially significant layer in April 2026. The methodology weights and sub-scores reflect this evidence base.

The entire programme publishes under CC-BY 4.0. Implementations are encouraged in any product, framework, or methodology that adopts the standards. The implementations directory in the standards repository lists conformant tools including competitors.

## The four-pillar architecture

The MakeitSEO Standard measures website performance across four pillars.

### Three outcome pillars

The three outcome pillars compose to the overall MakeitSEO score:

**SEO — Search Engine Optimisation.** Measures traditional search ranking on Google, Bing, and regional engines. The customer outcome is blue-link visibility for tracked queries. Detailed methodology in [`seo-scoring/v0.1`](../seo-scoring/v0.1.md).

**AEO — Answer Engine Optimisation.** Measures inclusion in deterministic answer surfaces — Google AI Overviews, featured snippets, knowledge panels, People Also Ask. The customer outcome is appearance in structured answer surfaces. Detailed methodology in [`aeo-scoring/v0.1`](../aeo-scoring/v0.1.md).

**GEO — Generative Engine Optimisation.** Measures inclusion as a cited or mentioned source in AI-generated responses across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, and Google AI Overviews. The customer outcome is AI citation. Detailed methodology in [`geo-scoring/v0.1`](../geo-scoring/v0.1.md).

Each pillar is independently scored on a 0–100 scale. The overall MakeitSEO score is the arithmetic mean of the three pillar scores:

```
overall_score = (seo_pillar_score + aeo_pillar_score + geo_pillar_score) / 3
```

The independence and mean-composition rules are documented in [`methodology-adr/v0.1`](../methodology-adr/v0.1.md), Decisions 1, 6, and 7.

### Agent Runtime Compatibility (ARC) — the measurement vocabulary

ARC is the fourth body of methodology in the programme but is **not a fourth outcome pillar**. ARC measures whether agent runtimes — the layer between AI models and websites — can read a site. Runtime compatibility is a precondition, not an outcome. It is measured within each outcome pillar via a runtime-compatibility sub-score.

ARC provides:

- A taxonomy of runtime categories (browser-execution, headless-fetch, model-native-harness)
- A characteristic vocabulary describing runtime behaviours
- An applicability matrix structure mapping checks to runtimes
- Per-(check × runtime) finding emission
- Routing rules from runtimes to outcome pillars via `relevant_outcome_pillar` characteristic
- A derived "Agent Runtime Readiness" view computed across pillar runtime sub-scores

Detailed methodology in [`agent-runtime-compatibility/v0.1`](../agent-runtime-compatibility/v0.1.md).

## What the methodology measures

### Customer outcomes

The three outcome pillars measure three distinct customer outcomes:

| Pillar | Outcome | How it surfaces |
|---|---|---|
| SEO | Traditional search ranking | Blue-link position in Google/Bing/regional results |
| AEO | Deterministic answer surface inclusion | Citation in AIO, featured snippet capture, knowledge panel population, PAA appearance |
| GEO | Generative AI citation | Citation in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, AIO responses |

A customer's overall presence in search and AI surfaces is captured by the composition of these three. Sites strong in SEO but weak in GEO are commercially exposed to the shift toward AI-mediated discovery; sites strong in GEO but weak in SEO miss the substantial click-traffic that still flows through traditional results. The framework's value is in making this multi-surface picture visible and measurable.

### Runtime readability

Beyond the three outcomes, the framework measures whether agent runtimes can read the site at all. Runtime invisibility is a precondition failure — a site that runtimes cannot fetch and parse cannot be cited, cannot appear in AI Overviews built from those runtimes' content, and may struggle with crawler-mediated indexing.

Runtime compatibility surfaces in three places:

- As a sub-score within each of the three outcome pillars (8% in SEO, 10% in AEO, 15% in GEO)
- As a per-(check × runtime) finding breakdown showing which specific runtimes can or cannot read the site
- As the derived "Agent Runtime Readiness" view, a cross-pillar diagnostic that summarises runtime visibility independently of pillar scoring

### Retrievability — cross-cutting derived view

The methodology also exposes "Retrievability" as a derived view computed across pillars. Retrievability is the property of a page being eligible to enter the candidate set that a search or AI system considers — distinct from rankability, which is the page's position within the candidate set once it has entered.

The term has acquired industry currency in 2026 as the candidate-set width assumed by SEO methodology has come into question. Court testimony from United States v. Google in October 2023 established that Google's deep-learning ranking components (RankBrain and successors) are applied to a candidate set of approximately 20-30 pages because evaluating more is computationally prohibitive. Hardware-economics shifts (Google's TurboQuant publication, March 2026; Pichai's Cheeky Pint discussion of memory supply constraints, April 2026) suggest the cost boundary that holds the candidate set narrow is now under pressure. Whether or not the boundary moves in 2026, retrievability is already the primary qualification gate for AI-mediated discovery — content the runtime cannot fetch and parse cannot be cited by AI engines regardless of its ranking quality.

The retrievability view is computed as the arithmetic mean of two components:

- **Agent Runtime Readiness** — itself a derived view computed across pillar runtime-compatibility sub-scores (covered above)
- **AI crawler accessibility** — the GEO pillar's crawler-permission sub-score (sub-score 7 in `geo-scoring/v0.1`)

```
retrievability_view = (
  agent_runtime_readiness +
  geo.ai_crawler_accessibility_subscore
) / 2
```

Like Agent Runtime Readiness, the retrievability view is a diagnostic. It is not a pillar score, and it does not compose into the overall MakeitSEO score (which remains the arithmetic mean of the three pillar scores). It is displayed alongside the three pillar scores on the customer dashboard, and is additionally surfaced within the SEO pillar's customer-facing UI because retrievability is increasingly material to traditional SEO outcomes as the search-engine candidate-set width evolves.

A page can rank well within current candidate sets and still score poorly on retrievability — for example a server-side-rendered marketing site with strong content quality but a robots.txt that blocks AI crawlers. The retrievability view surfaces this asymmetry where pillar scoring alone might obscure it.

**v0.2 forward path:** the v0.1 retrievability view measures runtime readability and crawler permission. It does not yet measure actual fetch evidence (whether AI crawlers are observed visiting the site in server logs over a recent window). A server-log analysis data adapter is identified as a v0.2 candidate; it would extend the retrievability view by adding a third component capturing observed-fetch-rate per AI crawler.

### Customer-facing surfaces

Customers see:

1. **Three pillar scores** — SEO, AEO, GEO, each 0–100. The arithmetic mean is the overall MakeitSEO score.
2. **Sub-score breakdowns within each pillar** — what's contributing to the score and what the improvement levers are.
3. **Agent Runtime Readiness diagnostic** — a cross-pillar view of runtime compatibility.
4. **Retrievability diagnostic** — a cross-pillar view summarising candidate-set eligibility, displayed alongside the pillar scores on the overall dashboard and within the SEO pillar detail.
5. **Per-(check × runtime), per-(check × surface), per-(platform × signal) finding breakdowns** — the granular level at which audit work happens.

The pattern across the framework is consistent: outcomes are scored, preconditions are surfaced as findings within outcomes, and the per-X breakdowns expose enough detail for diagnostic action.

## Composition rules

### Pillar score composition

Each pillar score is the weighted sum of its sub-scores. Weights sum to 100% within each pillar. Sub-score weights are documented per pillar spec.

```
seo_pillar_score   = sum(seo_sub_scores  × seo_weights)
aeo_pillar_score   = sum(aeo_sub_scores  × aeo_weights)
geo_pillar_score   = sum(geo_sub_scores  × geo_weights)
```

### Overall score composition

The overall MakeitSEO score is the arithmetic mean of the three pillar scores:

```
overall_score = (seo_pillar_score + aeo_pillar_score + geo_pillar_score) / 3
```

No weighting between pillars. The reasoning is captured in [`methodology-adr/v0.1`](../methodology-adr/v0.1.md), Decision 6.

### Independence

The three pillar scores are independent — no pillar is derived from or constrained by another. A customer can score 90/40/20 across SEO/AEO/GEO without internal contradiction; this picture is real and is honestly captured.

The independence rule is the central methodological commitment of the framework. It prevents the framework from hiding structural issues behind aggregate scoring (e.g. an "AI-era SEO" composite that masks GEO-specific weakness).

### Cascade weighting

ARC findings affect the runtime sub-score within each pillar through standard weighted-sum composition. Whether ARC failures should additionally cascade into the other sub-scores within a pillar (e.g. depress citation rate beyond the runtime sub-score effect when runtimes cannot read the site) is deferred to v0.2 per [`methodology-adr/v0.1`](../methodology-adr/v0.1.md), Deferred 2.

The platform collects time-paired ARC × outcome data from launch to enable this v0.2 review on evidence rather than speculation.

## What the methodology does not measure

A standards programme is improved by being honest about its limits. The MakeitSEO Standard at v0.1 does not measure:

- **Paid search performance.** SEO scope is organic visibility only. Customers running paid campaigns track those separately.
- **Sentiment in AI-generated mentions.** Whether a brand is described positively, negatively, or neutrally in AI responses is captured as finding-level context but is not a sub-score. The HubSpot/Peec/Profound treatment of sentiment as a score component is acknowledged; the MakeitSEO position is that sentiment is more useful as a finding detail than a score driver at v0.1.
- **Conversion attribution from AI-mediated traffic.** Customer GA4 or analytics integration is supported for traffic attribution; conversion attribution from AI-referred sessions is an implementation concern, not a methodology concern.
- **Tool-calling protocol compatibility (MCP, etc.).** MCP defines how agents invoke structured operations against a service; the MakeitSEO Standard measures whether agent runtimes can *read* a website. These are different layers. ARC explicitly excludes MCP compatibility from scope.
- **Brand sentiment in social media or community spaces independent of AI responses.** Brand sentiment in Reddit threads or Twitter conversations is measured indirectly (as part of the brand web mentions sub-score quality weighting) but not as a standalone signal.
- **Adversarial AI engine behaviour.** Cases where AI engines deliberately misrepresent or refuse to cite a brand are out of scope. The methodology measures what AI engines do; remedial action against engine behaviour is product-specific.

## Versioning conventions

The programme follows RFC-style path versioning. Specifications are published at versions of the form `spec-name/vX.Y`.

- **v0.X** versions are drafts. Stable enough to publish and adopt, subject to revision based on first-cohort implementation data.
- **v1.0** is the first non-draft version. Suitable for citation as an authoritative reference.
- **vX.Y** with X >= 1 are stable versions. Breaking changes require explicit migration paths.

Findings produced under a specific spec version carry the `methodology_ref = 'spec-name/vX.Y'` field. When specs are promoted, existing findings retain their original version.

The current programme version is **v0.1**. All constituent specs publish at v0.1. The v0.1 → v0.2 promotion is expected ~60–90 days post-MakeitSEO platform launch.

## Frequently asked questions

### Why three pillars rather than four?

The earlier internal framing treated ARC as a fourth pillar. The 11 May 2026 review concluded that ARC measures a precondition rather than an outcome and should not compose as a peer pillar with three outcome pillars. The reasoning is captured in [`methodology-adr/v0.1`](../methodology-adr/v0.1.md), Decisions 2 and 3.

ARC remains a first-class body of methodology — it has its own spec, its own taxonomy, its own measurement framework — but it is not a pillar in the score composition. Runtime compatibility is measured within each outcome pillar where its failure mode lands.

### Why is FAQ schema not in AEO?

Google deprecated FAQ rich results for all sites on 7 May 2026. The AEO sub-score for FAQ schema coverage was measuring a contribution to a SERP outcome (the FAQ rich result) that no longer exists for the vast majority of sites. Continuing to score FAQ as an AEO signal would mean awarding points for a contribution that produces no measurable outcome.

FAQ-structured content remains useful for AI citation and is captured in [`geo-scoring/v0.1`](../geo-scoring/v0.1.md) under structured-content sub-scoring at modest weight.

### Why is the overall score the arithmetic mean rather than a weighted sum?

The arithmetic mean has a built-in defence: we measure three outcomes, they're all important, we give them equal weight because the evidence base does not support a confident claim that one outcome intrinsically matters more than the others.

Unequal pillar weighting would require defending specific numbers. The current evidence base does not support a confident assignment. Sub-score weights within each pillar are evidence-grounded; pillar-level weights are not, so they are equal.

This may change in v0.2 if cohort data supports a confident weighting.

### How does this compare to Profound, Peec, Otterly, Rankshift?

Those tools measure GEO outcomes (citation rate, mention share, sentiment) using prompt-based monitoring of AI engines. The MakeitSEO Standard incorporates similar measurements in the GEO pillar (citation rate, mention share) but goes further:

- It measures precursor signals (brand mentions, freshness, per-platform affinity) that the outcome-only tools do not address
- It measures the full SEO and AEO outcome layers, not just GEO
- It introduces the runtime compatibility measurement vocabulary

The MakeitSEO platform competes on execution; the standards programme competes on methodology breadth and rigour. Outcome-only tools are not in conflict with the standard; they are partial implementations of the GEO pillar.

### How does this compare to Cloudflare's AI Agent Readiness Tool and isitagentready.com?

Those tools measure a subset of what ARC v0.1 measures, primarily focused on technical compliance (structured data, robots.txt rules, agent-skills JSON). ARC v0.1 includes:

- A formal runtime taxonomy (browser-execution, headless-fetch, model-native-harness)
- An applicability matrix structure
- Per-(check × runtime) finding emission with explicit pillar routing
- A derived cross-pillar Agent Runtime Readiness view

The adjacent tools are complementary to ARC, not competing with it. They are referenced in the ARC v0.1 references section.

### What is Retrievability and why is it surfaced as a derived view?

Retrievability is the property of a page being eligible to enter the candidate set that a search or AI system considers — distinct from rankability (the position within the candidate set once entered). The term acquired industry currency in 2026 as the candidate-set width assumed by SEO methodology came into question (Google's TurboQuant publication, Pichai's Cheeky Pint discussion of retrieval economics, broader industry coverage including Jeffrey 2026 in Search Engine Land).

The retrievability concept overlaps roughly two-thirds with ARC (runtime readability) and one-third with the GEO pillar's AI crawler accessibility sub-score. Promoting it to a new pillar would duplicate ARC; promoting it to a new SEO sub-score would either require weight redistribution (a methodology change unjustified by current evidence) or be additive to 100% (mathematically invalid). The derived view honours the industry framing without restructuring the architecture.

The decision is captured in [`methodology-adr/v0.1`](../methodology-adr/v0.1.md), Decision 15.
