## Abstract

This specification defines how content freshness is measured for use as an input to scoring pillars in the MakeitSEO Standards Programme. Content freshness is the age and update cadence of pages — a property that 2026 evidence shows materially affects both traditional search ranking and AI citation likelihood.

Freshness is consumed as a sub-score by two pillars: `seo-scoring/v0.1` at 10% weight, and `geo-scoring/v0.1` at 10% weight. This spec defines the measurement procedure; pillar specs define how the resulting measurement contributes to pillar scoring.

The 2026 evidence base for treating freshness as a primary signal:

- **ConvertMate 80M+ citation analysis (2026):** content updated within 30 days receives 3.2× more AI citations across platforms
- **Perplexity-specific data (2026):** content updated within 2 hours sees a 38% boost in citation probability for Perplexity
- **Google ranking system documentation (long-established):** freshness weighting is explicit for time-sensitive query categories (news, current events, evolving topics)

The combination of search-ranking and AI-citation evidence supports first-class measurement of freshness at v0.1 rather than burying it inside a generic content-quality assessment.

---

## Status

This is the v0.1 draft of content freshness measurement. The multiplier curve specified is grounded in the 2026 evidence base but not yet calibrated against MakeitSEO's own customer audit data. v0.2 (planned ~Month 2-3 post-launch) may refine the curve against cohort outcomes.

The spec is intentionally minimal at v0.1. The measurement procedure is straightforward and the curve is the only meaningful methodological choice; future expansion will likely address per-vertical curve calibration (news sites may need a steeper curve; evergreen reference sites may need a flatter one) and the question of programmatic freshness (sites that touch `dateModified` without changing content).

## Scope

### In scope

- The freshness multiplier curve at v0.1
- Detection methodology for `datePublished` and `dateModified` signals
- Fallback ordering when primary signals are absent
- Aggregation rules for per-page freshness into per-domain freshness
- Edge cases identified at v0.1

### Out of scope

- Per-vertical curve calibration (deferred to v0.2 on cohort evidence)
- Detection of programmatic freshness gaming (touching `dateModified` without genuine content change; deferred)
- Per-query freshness sensitivity (a feature of how pillar specs *use* freshness, not of how it's measured)
- Time-sensitive vs evergreen content classification (a content-quality property, not a freshness measurement)

## Definitions

### Content age

The interval, in days, between the most recent of (`dateModified`, `datePublished`, HTTP `Last-Modified` header) and the time of measurement.

### Freshness multiplier

A value in the range [0.30, 1.00] indicating the freshness contribution of a single page. Applied per page; aggregated to per-domain.

### Multiplier curve

The mapping from content age to freshness multiplier. The curve at v0.1 is a discrete step function; v0.2 may smooth it into a continuous curve if cohort data warrants.

### Page importance weighting

The relative weight assigned to a page when aggregating per-page freshness into per-domain freshness. Pages with higher commercial relevance (citation-receiving pages, tracked pages) carry more weight than orphan pages. Specific weights are implementation-defined; this spec specifies that the aggregation is weighted, not that the weights are uniform.

---

## Multiplier curve at v0.1

The curve is discrete and additive across five age bands:

| Content age | Freshness multiplier |
|---|---|
| 0–30 days | 1.00 |
| 30–90 days | 0.85 |
| 90–180 days | 0.65 |
| 180–365 days | 0.45 |
| > 365 days | 0.30 |

The thresholds are evidence-aligned to the ConvertMate finding (3.2× multiplier under 30 days) and to standard SEO guidance on freshness cycles for non-news content (90-day refresh is common; 6-month-to-annual refresh is standard for evergreen pages). The numeric multipliers reflect a sensible decay curve: full credit for very recent content, modest decay over the first three months, steeper decay thereafter.

### Why a step function rather than a continuous curve

A continuous curve (exponential decay) would be more elegant. A step function is more transparent: customers can identify exactly which band their page is in, and operators can validate that the implementation matches the spec without running interpolation math.

v0.2 may move to a continuous curve once cohort data confirms that the step boundaries match observed citation behaviour. The transition will be a non-breaking refinement; methodology version refs (`content-freshness/v0.1` → `content-freshness/v0.2`) ensure interpretability across versions.

## Measurement procedure

### Per-page freshness detection

For each measured page, the implementation:

1. **Attempts to extract `dateModified` from JSON-LD schema.** Schema types in priority order: `Article`, `BlogPosting`, `NewsArticle`, `WebPage`. If present and validly parseable as a date, use it.
2. **If `dateModified` is absent, attempts to extract `datePublished` from the same schema types.** If present and validly parseable, use it.
3. **If schema dates are absent, falls back to the HTTP `Last-Modified` response header.** Honour the header value if present.
4. **If all of the above are absent, the page is recorded as `freshness_age_days = NULL` with `freshness_multiplier = NULL`.** The page is excluded from the domain aggregate but its absence is logged so customers can be alerted to the missing signal.

### Multiplier assignment

Once content age is known, the multiplier is assigned by the curve table above. The assignment is deterministic.

### Per-domain aggregation

The per-domain freshness score is the weighted mean of per-page multipliers across the tracked-page set:

```
domain_freshness = (
  sum(page_multiplier × page_importance_weight) / sum(page_importance_weight)
) × 100
```

The result is a 0–100 score consumable by pillar scoring.

Pages with `freshness_multiplier = NULL` (no date detected) are excluded from the numerator and denominator. If more than 30% of tracked pages have no detectable date, the domain aggregate is flagged `low_confidence = true` and a finding is emitted advising the operator to add date signals to the affected pages.

## Implementation guidance

### Detection priority

The strict priority order (`dateModified` schema → `datePublished` schema → `Last-Modified` header) is important. `dateModified` more accurately reflects the *current* freshness of the content; `datePublished` reflects when the content was first authored, which can be much older than the latest meaningful update; `Last-Modified` headers are often automated and may not reflect content changes.

Implementations should not fall back from schema to `Last-Modified` if schema parsing fails for a structural reason (malformed JSON-LD). The structural failure is itself a finding ("schema markup present but malformed") and should be surfaced to the customer.

### Date format handling

Implementations should accept ISO 8601 format (`2026-05-11T14:30:00Z`) as the primary format. Common variants (date-only, RFC 2822, locale-specific formats) are tolerated where unambiguous. Genuinely ambiguous dates (e.g. `01/02/2026` could be 1 Feb or 2 Jan depending on locale) are treated as parse failures.

### Caching and refresh

Per-page freshness signals do not need to be re-fetched on every audit. The implementation may cache `dateModified` and `Last-Modified` values for a reasonable period (e.g. 24 hours per audit cycle) and only re-fetch when the audit pipeline indicates the page content has changed (via existing change-detection signals).

### Methodology version tagging

Every freshness measurement carries `methodology_ref = 'content-freshness/v0.1'`. Findings consumed by SEO and GEO pillar scoring may carry dual references (`content-freshness/v0.1+seo-scoring/v0.1` or `content-freshness/v0.1+geo-scoring/v0.1`).

## Edge cases

### Pages with no detectable date

A page with no JSON-LD schema, no `Last-Modified` header, and no other date signal is recorded as `freshness_age_days = NULL`. The implementation surfaces this as a finding ("date signal missing") so the customer can act on it. The page is excluded from the per-domain freshness aggregate.

### Pages with programmatically-touched `dateModified`

Some CMS implementations touch `dateModified` on every page render or on cache regeneration without actual content change. v0.1 cannot distinguish this from genuine updates. The aggregate may overstate freshness for sites affected.

v0.2 will introduce a content-change verification step: if `dateModified` advances without a measurable change in the page's primary content hash, the freshness signal is dampened. This is deliberately deferred to v0.2 because the content-hash diff requires per-page state retention beyond v0.1's scope.

### Pages with `dateModified` in the future

A page reporting `dateModified` in the future of the measurement time is treated as a parse-failure-equivalent: the page is recorded as `freshness_age_days = NULL` and a finding is emitted ("future-dated content detected"). This catches both genuine errors and gaming attempts.

### Pages with `datePublished` later than `dateModified`

A page where the schema reports `datePublished` later than `dateModified` is treated as malformed schema. The implementation prefers `dateModified` per the priority order, logs the schema inconsistency as a finding, but does not down-weight the freshness score.

### Genuinely evergreen reference content

Some content is genuinely stable (canonical reference pages, glossaries, historical records) and should not be penalised for not being updated. v0.1 does not distinguish evergreen from stale content; the multiplier curve applies uniformly. v0.2 may introduce an explicit "evergreen tag" mechanism via schema markup (e.g. a `evergreen: true` extension) that exempts marked pages from the freshness penalty.

### Time-zone handling

Date signals are normalised to UTC for age calculation. Dates without explicit timezone information (e.g. ISO 8601 dates with no Z suffix and no offset) are interpreted as UTC. Implementations should document this in finding details where it materially affects the per-page age band.

## Examples

### Example 1: SaaS blog, monthly update cadence

A B2B SaaS blog with 40 articles, average update cadence ~6 weeks per page. Most recent article 3 days old; oldest cycled to refresh 4 weeks ago.

- 35 pages have `dateModified` < 30 days → multiplier 1.00
- 4 pages have `dateModified` 30–90 days → multiplier 0.85
- 1 page has `dateModified` 90–180 days → multiplier 0.65

Weighted mean (assuming uniform importance weight): `(35 × 1.00 + 4 × 0.85 + 1 × 0.65) / 40 = 0.974`

Domain freshness score: 97.

### Example 2: Marketing site, annual major refresh

A B2C marketing site with 12 primary pages, all touched in the last annual cycle. Mix of recent product launches and older brand pages.

- 3 product pages have `dateModified` < 30 days → multiplier 1.00
- 4 pages have `dateModified` 30–90 days → multiplier 0.85
- 3 pages have `dateModified` 90–180 days → multiplier 0.65
- 2 pages have `dateModified` 180–365 days → multiplier 0.45

Weighted mean: `(3 × 1.00 + 4 × 0.85 + 3 × 0.65 + 2 × 0.45) / 12 = 0.713`

Domain freshness score: 71.

### Example 3: Reference site with no date signals

A reference glossary site with 200 pages, no JSON-LD schema, no `Last-Modified` headers.

- All 200 pages have `freshness_age_days = NULL` → excluded from aggregate
- Coverage is 0/200; > 30% threshold triggered

Domain freshness score: `low_confidence = true`, score reported as `null` with a "date signals missing" finding.

The customer is advised to add `dateModified` to JSON-LD schema or to configure their CMS to emit `Last-Modified` headers.
