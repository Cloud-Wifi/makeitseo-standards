## Abstract

This specification defines how brand web mentions are measured for use as an input to scoring pillars in the MakeitSEO Standards Programme. A brand web mention is any mention of a brand name (or registered variants) anywhere on the open web, with or without a clickable link to the brand's domain.

The 2026 evidence base elevates brand mentions to the strongest single off-domain predictor of AI citation:

- **Ahrefs 75K-brand study (2026):** brand web mentions correlate with Google AI Overview citation at **r = 0.664** — the strongest single predictor identified
- **Digital Bloom 680M-citation analysis (2025):** brand search volume (a related but distinct signal) correlates at r = 0.334
- **Evertune 75K-brand parallel study (2026):** brand mentions outperform backlinks as a predictor of LLM citation across all four major AI engines
- **Google March 2026 Core Update:** unstructured brand mentions formally recognised as authority signals alongside traditional backlinks

Mentions are consumed as sub-scores by two pillars: `geo-scoring/v0.1` at 15% weight, and `seo-scoring/v0.1` at 3% weight. This spec defines the measurement procedure; pillar specs define how the resulting measurement contributes to pillar scoring.

The asymmetric weighting (15% in GEO, 3% in SEO) reflects the evidence base: mentions correlate strongly with AI citation, modestly with traditional ranking. Methodology v0.1 honours this asymmetry rather than smoothing it.

---

## Status

This is the v0.1 draft of brand web mentions measurement. The tier-based quality weighting and log-scaled normalisation are grounded in the 2026 evidence base but not yet calibrated against MakeitSEO's own customer audit data. v0.2 (planned ~Month 2-3 post-launch) may refine the quality tiers and the normalisation curve.

The spec is intentionally minimal at v0.1. The principal methodological choices are: (a) what counts as a quality-tier-1 source, (b) how mentions scale into a 0–100 score. Both are documented here; both are candidates for v0.2 refinement.

## Scope

### In scope

- Detection methodology for brand mentions (linked and unlinked) on the open web
- Quality-tier classification of mention sources
- Tier-based weighting formula
- Log-scaled normalisation to a 0–100 score
- Edge cases identified at v0.1

### Out of scope

- Sentiment analysis of mentions (mention quality is treated as source-authority-based at v0.1; sentiment is a v0.2 candidate)
- Brand name disambiguation strategies (treated as implementation-specific; consumed via the `business_contexts` brand-variant capture rather than spec'd here)
- Social-media mention volume (Reddit and similar fall under per-platform source affinity in `per-platform-source-affinity/v0.1`; this spec covers open-web mentions generally)
- Branded search volume (a related signal; treated as a quality-weighting input rather than a standalone measurement)

## Definitions

### Brand web mention

Any occurrence of the customer's brand name (canonical form or registered variant) on the open web, in textual content. A mention may be:

- **Linked** — the mention is a hyperlink to the customer's domain (overlaps with traditional backlinks)
- **Unlinked** — the mention is plain text with no clickable link to the customer's domain

Both types contribute to the brand mentions measurement. The 2026 evidence base supports treating both forms similarly; the unlinked form is the larger volume and the more recently recognised signal (Google March 2026 Core Update).

### Brand variant

A registered alternative form of the customer's brand name. Examples: official name + abbreviation; legal entity name + trade name; original spelling + common misspelling. The implementation captures variants during onboarding and queries them collectively when detecting mentions.

### Quality tier

A classification of the source domain hosting the mention. v0.1 defines three tiers based on Domain Authority and an explicit allowlist of high-authority publications. The tier of the source affects the mention's contribution to the weighted count.

### Weighted mention count

A computed quantity that aggregates raw mention counts across quality tiers using v0.1 tier weights. The weighted count is then log-scaled to produce the final brand mentions score.

### Mention quality

The qualitative property of a mention measured by source authority. A mention in a Tier 1 publication (e.g. Forbes, TechCrunch, BBC, FT, industry trade press) is treated as higher quality than a mention in a Tier 3 source (e.g. small personal blog, low-DA forum).

---

## Quality tiers at v0.1

The implementation classifies each mention by the source domain hosting the mention.

### Tier 1 — High authority

A source qualifies as Tier 1 if **either** of the following applies:

- The source domain has Domain Authority (DA) ≥ 60 per a reputable DA provider (Moz, Ahrefs DR, or equivalent)
- The source domain is on the operator-maintained allowlist of recognised authority publications (e.g. major national press, recognised industry trade press, established universities, governmental and inter-governmental publications)

The allowlist supplements DA because some legitimate Tier 1 sources (e.g. specialised research publications, regional newspapers, niche-but-authoritative trade journals) have DA values that under-represent their authority. The allowlist is operator-curated and updated as new sources are identified.

### Tier 2 — Mid authority

A source qualifies as Tier 2 if it has Domain Authority 30 ≤ DA < 60 and is not on the Tier 1 allowlist.

### Tier 3 — Low authority

A source qualifies as Tier 3 if it has Domain Authority < 30.

### Quality-tier overrides

Two operator-configurable overrides apply:

- **Tier 1 allowlist** — promotes specific domains from their DA-derived tier
- **Tier 3 denylist** — explicitly excludes mentions from sources that are evidently spam (link farms, scraper sites, content-mill networks)

Denylisted mentions are not counted in any tier. Allowlisted mentions are counted as Tier 1 regardless of DA.

## Weighting formula

The weighted mention count is:

```
weighted_count = tier_1_count × 3.0
              + tier_2_count × 1.5
              + tier_3_count × 0.5
```

The weights reflect the evidence-base finding that source authority materially affects the citation-correlation strength of a mention. A Tier 1 mention contributes 6× the weight of a Tier 3 mention.

The weights are deliberately chosen to be transparent (whole and half-integer multipliers); they are not the only defensible weights. v0.2 may calibrate them against cohort data if a different distribution proves better-correlated with measured citation outcomes.

---

## Normalisation curve

The weighted count is normalised to a 0–100 score via a log-scaled formula:

```
brand_mentions_score = log10(weighted_count + 1) × 25
```

Capped at 100. The `+1` prevents `log10(0)` from being undefined for domains with no mentions; the multiplier `× 25` is calibrated such that a typical mid-market brand with ~200 quality-weighted mentions over 90 days scores around 58, and a very-established brand with ~10,000 quality-weighted mentions scores around 100 (capped).

### Why log-scaled

A linear scale would produce extreme values for high-volume mention domains. A Wikipedia-listed brand with millions of mentions would score 1,000,000× while a small startup with two mentions would score 2×. The score range becomes uninterpretable.

Log scaling compresses the range: a 10× difference in mention volume produces a constant additive difference in score. This preserves the directional signal (more mentions → higher score) while keeping scores in a meaningful 0–100 range comparable to other sub-scores.

### Calibration commitment

The `× 25` multiplier and the normalisation shape are evidence-aligned but not cohort-calibrated. v0.2 reviews this against actual MakeitSEO customer audit data and adjusts if the typical-customer scoring distribution is materially skewed.

## Measurement procedure

### Data source

The primary data source is the DataForSEO Backlinks API's unlinked-mention endpoint, which returns mentions of a brand name across the open web indexed by DataForSEO. The query is configured with the customer's brand name and registered variants.

The DataForSEO Backlinks API is selected for v0.1 because (a) it is the existing platform integration, (b) it provides per-mention source metadata enabling tier classification, (c) it supports unlinked mentions which are the larger volume and the v0.1-relevant signal.

Alternative data sources (e.g. Brand24, Mention.com, Brandwatch) are not used at v0.1 because the existing platform integration is sufficient and adding redundant sources would increase per-customer cost without proportional value. v0.2 may introduce additional sources for cross-validation if cohort data shows DataForSEO under-represents certain mention categories.

### Query period

The standard measurement window is **the prior 90 days**. This balances:

- Recency — older mentions are less correlated with current citation likelihood
- Stability — a 30-day window is too narrow and produces noisy week-to-week variation
- Industry alignment — 90 days is the typical window for brand-mention analysis in marketing reporting

The window is configurable per implementation; 90 days is the default and the reference for cross-customer comparison.

### Tier classification

For each detected mention:

1. Extract the source domain
2. If the source domain is on the operator-maintained denylist, exclude the mention
3. If the source domain is on the operator-maintained Tier 1 allowlist, classify as Tier 1
4. Otherwise look up the source domain's Domain Authority via the existing DA data adapter
5. Classify per the DA thresholds (Tier 1: DA ≥ 60; Tier 2: 30 ≤ DA < 60; Tier 3: DA < 30)

Tier classification is deterministic given the DA value and the allowlist/denylist state at the time of measurement.

### Aggregation

The per-tier counts are aggregated to produce the weighted count and then normalised per the formula above.

### Cadence

The brand mentions measurement runs on a tier-aware cadence per the implementation:

- **Starter tier:** monthly refresh
- **Growth tier:** weekly refresh
- **Scale tier:** weekly refresh

The signal does not change daily; daily polling is wasteful. The tier-aware cadence balances signal freshness against API cost.

### Methodology version tagging

Every brand mention measurement carries `methodology_ref = 'brand-web-mentions/v0.1'`. Findings consumed by SEO and GEO pillar scoring may carry dual references (`brand-web-mentions/v0.1+seo-scoring/v0.1` or `brand-web-mentions/v0.1+geo-scoring/v0.1`).

## Implementation guidance

### Brand variant capture

Implementations should capture brand variants during customer onboarding (in `business_contexts` or equivalent). Variants should include:

- Canonical brand name (most common spelling)
- Alternative spellings (e.g. "Make It SEO", "MakeItSEO", "Makeitseo")
- Legal entity name if different from trade name
- Common misspellings if known
- Localised variants if relevant (translations or transliterations)

The detection query uses the union of all variants.

### Allowlist and denylist maintenance

The Tier 1 allowlist and Tier 3 denylist are operator-maintained. v0.1 ships with a starter allowlist seeded with widely-recognised authority publications across the major customer markets (US, UK, EU, ZA per the MakeitSEO customer geography). v0.2 may automate parts of allowlist maintenance via authority-score thresholds, but v0.1 keeps it manual to maintain operator control over what counts as Tier 1.

### Storage

Per-domain brand mention snapshots are stored as time-series rows (one per measurement cycle). This enables:

- Trend visualisation in customer-facing UI
- Cascade-monitoring analyses linking mention volume to citation outcomes
- Operator forensics if a customer's score moves unexpectedly

### Raw payload retention

The DataForSEO response payload is retained as JSONB for forensics. Retention period is 90 days by default; longer retention is operator-configurable.

## Edge cases

### New brands with no mentions yet

A brand launched in the last 90 days will have very low mention volume. Score: low, accurately. The score is honestly low; customer-facing finding contextualises this ("early-stage brand; mention growth typically follows 6–18 month trajectory").

No score adjustment is made for brand age. The customer's growth path is brand-building activity over time, not score interpretation.

### Brand name collisions

A brand sharing its name with a more famous entity (e.g. a small SaaS named "Apple") will produce mention queries with high noise. v0.1 does not implement automated collision detection; the implementation relies on operator inspection of the raw mention payload to identify and exclude obvious-collision mentions.

v0.2 may introduce automated collision detection (e.g. semantic clustering of mention contexts to separate the small SaaS from the Cupertino corporation), but at v0.1 the responsibility is operator-side.

### Foreign-language mentions

Mentions in languages other than the customer's primary market language are counted at face value. A UK customer with German-language mentions has those mentions contributing equally to the weighted count.

This is a deliberate v0.1 choice. AI engines retrieve and cite across languages; an English-speaking US user querying ChatGPT in English may receive a citation from a German-language source if the engine deems it relevant. Down-weighting foreign-language mentions would under-represent the customer's actual AI-citation surface.

v0.2 may introduce language-aware weighting if cohort data shows the assumption is wrong.

### Tier 1 allowlist gaming

A determined adversary could attempt to seed mentions in Tier 1 allowlisted publications via paid placement or PR-as-service relationships. This is the same gaming surface that traditional backlink-buying targets; the measurement does not specifically defend against it.

The measurement reflects what's on the open web. If a customer has paid placements in Tier 1 publications, those mentions count. The measurement does not attempt to detect or down-weight paid placements at v0.1.

This is a known limitation. v0.2 may introduce paid-content detection (e.g. parsing for `rel="sponsored"` markers, sponsored-content indicators) but the gaming-detection arms race is not v0.1's principal concern.

### Mention without brand-context match

DataForSEO's unlinked-mention endpoint returns mentions of the brand string regardless of context. A mention of "MakeitSEO" in a sentence like "we considered MakeitSEO and three competitors" counts. A mention in a sentence like "we tested make it SEO friendly" might match if the variant matching is loose.

v0.1 relies on DataForSEO's matching algorithm and tolerates the false-positive rate that results. The raw payload is retained so operators can audit and refine the allowlist/denylist as false-positive patterns emerge.

## Examples

### Example 1: Mid-market B2B SaaS, established brand

A B2B SaaS with consistent industry coverage over the last 90 days.

- Tier 1 mentions: 12 (industry trade publications, two TechCrunch features)
- Tier 2 mentions: 84 (smaller trade blogs, industry directories, podcast show notes)
- Tier 3 mentions: 230 (forum mentions, personal blogs, small newsletters)

Weighted count: `12 × 3.0 + 84 × 1.5 + 230 × 0.5 = 36 + 126 + 115 = 277`

Brand mentions score: `log10(277 + 1) × 25 = log10(278) × 25 ≈ 2.444 × 25 = 61.1`

The score is 61. The mid-market profile produces a mid-range score; the Tier 1 mentions contribute disproportionately to the weighted count despite being only 12 of 326 mentions.

### Example 2: Local services business

A local services business with limited off-domain footprint.

- Tier 1 mentions: 1 (single regional newspaper feature)
- Tier 2 mentions: 8 (local directory listings, community blogs)
- Tier 3 mentions: 35 (review forum mentions, neighbourhood social posts)

Weighted count: `1 × 3.0 + 8 × 1.5 + 35 × 0.5 = 3 + 12 + 17.5 = 32.5`

Brand mentions score: `log10(32.5 + 1) × 25 = log10(33.5) × 25 ≈ 1.525 × 25 = 38.1`

The score is 38. The local business profile produces an expectedly low score; growth path is local PR and community-content investment.

### Example 3: Major consumer brand

A well-established consumer brand with broad press coverage.

- Tier 1 mentions: 450 (national press, major trade press, international coverage)
- Tier 2 mentions: 2,100 (regional press, industry analysis, podcast mentions)
- Tier 3 mentions: 15,000 (forum mentions, social-adjacent web, long tail)

Weighted count: `450 × 3.0 + 2,100 × 1.5 + 15,000 × 0.5 = 1,350 + 3,150 + 7,500 = 12,000`

Brand mentions score: `log10(12,000 + 1) × 25 ≈ 4.079 × 25 = 101.98`

Capped at 100.

The score is 100 (capped). The cap is reached around weighted count ≈ 10,000; brands above this threshold are all scored 100, with the Agent Runtime Readiness and other pillar sub-scores providing differentiation between very-established brands.
